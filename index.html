Strategic Proposal
OKR vs KPI — Why the Difference Matters
Understanding the right framework before committing to one
The core challenge at Vaudit
"Every team can be occupied and productive. That does not mean the company is moving in the right direction."
The Real Problem — When KPIs Become Someone Else's Job
A common failure pattern in growing companies — and how to fix it structurally.
PATTERN IDENTIFIED
Division leads are not maintaining their own KPIs — HR is filling the gap
What is happening

KPIs are supposed to be owned and updated by each division lead — but in practice, HR ends up doing the updates at the end of the cycle. Division leads are not maintaining visibility into their own team's performance numbers.

This is not simply a communication problem. It is a system design problem. When there is no consequence for not updating and someone else always fills the gap, the rational behaviour is to let that someone else handle it.

Why it happens
  • →KPI updates are a manual, low-urgency task — always pushed to tomorrow
  • →No visible consequence when a division lead skips the update — HR absorbs it
  • →Leadership does not see in real time whose numbers are stale — so no one gets asked
  • →The system requires manual discipline — and discipline alone never scales
Ops Manager Role
How Operations removes the bottleneck — without becoming the enforcer

The Operations Manager does not own the communication problem directly — but owns the system that makes accountability visible. The fix is not to send reminders or chase division leads. The fix is to build a system where not updating has a visible cost and updating requires no extra effort.

Remove the manual step

The Ops-Bot (O1) connects to all data sources — Zendesk, Google Sheets, the Dashboard — and updates KR progress automatically. Division leads do not need to manually enter anything.

Make staleness visible

The weekly OKR digest (O3) shows leadership exactly which division's numbers are current and which are stale. The conversation shifts from "why didn't HR update?" to "why is Division X still amber after 2 weeks?"

Shift ownership clearly

Each Key Result in the OKR module has a named owner. Leadership assigns it, the system tracks it. Ops builds and maintains the infrastructure — but the accountability sits with the division, not HR.

Key distinction OKRs do not fix communication by themselves. They fix the system around communication — making ownership explicit, progress automatic, and gaps visible to leadership before they become problems. HR stops being the fallback because there is nothing left to fall back on.
OKR vs KPI — Side by Side
Both are useful — but they answer different questions. Here is how they differ in practice.
Dimension KPI (Key Performance Indicator) OKR (Objectives & Key Results)
Purpose Monitor ongoing health of existing processes. "Are we maintaining performance?" Drive change and ambitious progress. "Are we moving toward a new goal?"
Time horizon Ongoing — tracked weekly or monthly indefinitely Time-boxed — typically quarterly or semi-annually
Who sets it Usually set by function heads to measure team performance Set by leadership as company direction, cascaded to teams
What it measures Operational output: ticket volume, resolution time, revenue per period Progress toward a specific outcome: "Finance invoicing is fully automated"
Target score 100% is good — you want to hit the target every time 60–70% is good — OKRs should be ambitious enough that 100% means the target was set too low
Risk if overused Teams optimise for the metric, not the outcome. You hit the number but miss the goal. Too many OKRs dilute focus. 3 objectives maximum per team per cycle.
Best used for SLA compliance, ticket resolution rates, uptime, revenue per product Strategic initiatives: launching automation, building new capabilities, improving cross-team alignment
Vaudit use case Dashboard metrics: open cases, SLA breach rate, PO approval time Jul–Dec objectives: Ops-Bot intelligence layer, Finance pipeline, Ops Automation Expansion

Why OKRs Work for Vaudit Right Now
Six concrete reasons to adopt OKRs over KPI-only tracking at this stage of company growth.
🎯

Direction over activity

OKRs force every team to ask "is this work connected to a company goal?" — not just "am I busy?" A team can hit all KPIs and still miss the board target.

👁️

Visible to leadership

With OKRs in the dashboard, Mark and Michael see progress in real time — without waiting for a weekly meeting or a status update.

🔗

Connects to board targets

Company OKRs set by leadership cascade to teams. Every deliverable traces back to what was committed to the Board — no gap between execution and governance.

⚡

Faster course correction

An amber OKR in week 4 is recoverable. A missed board target in week 12 is not. OKRs surface problems early enough to act.

🤝

Cross-division alignment

When Finance, Ops, and Partnerships share a company-level objective, they collaborate toward it — instead of optimising for their own numbers in isolation.

? Is OKR the right fit for a company of 40–50 people?

Yes — and actually, a company at this size is exactly where OKRs deliver the most value. Here is why.

Why OKRs work at this stage
  • ✓ Small enough that 3–5 company objectives can actually represent everything important
  • ✓ Large enough that without alignment, different divisions start pulling in different directions
  • ✓ Fast-moving enough that a quarterly check-in keeps strategy current without bureaucracy
  • ✓ This is when habits form — companies that build OKR culture at 40 people maintain it at 400
Common mistakes at startup scale
  • ✗ Too many OKRs — 3 company objectives max. If everything is a priority, nothing is.
  • ✗ Treating OKRs like a task list — they should describe outcomes, not activities
  • ✗ Scoring too harshly — a 70% OKR is a win if the target was ambitious. 100% means it was too easy.
  • ✗ Setting OKRs once and never reviewing — a brief monthly check-in per team is enough
Recommendation for Vaudit Start with 3 company-level OKRs set by Mark and Michael for Jul–Dec 2026. Each division then defines how their work contributes to those 3 objectives — not a separate set of divisional OKRs, but Key Results that tie directly back to the top-level objectives. The dashboard module (O3 in this plan) makes this visible automatically.

Proposal: OKR Module in the Vaudit Accountability Dashboard

Rather than tracking OKRs in a separate spreadsheet, the proposal is to build an OKR module inside the Vaudit Accountability Dashboard — where cases, SLAs, and escalations already live. Key Results update automatically from real operational data, not from manual self-reporting.

▦
Leadership sets company Objectives Mark and Michael define the direction each quarter. Objectives cascade to divisions as assigned Key Results.
⟳
Auto-tracking from live data Case closures, SLA compliance, and task completions automatically update KR progress — no manual entry needed.
📬
Weekly Slack digest to leadership Every Monday: which objectives are on track, which are at risk, what is blocked.
📄
One-click board export A board-ready summary generated directly from the OKR dashboard — no formatting work before a board meeting.
